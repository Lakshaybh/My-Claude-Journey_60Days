Acquisition offer vs. staying independent

A structured breakdown of a $40M acquisition offer against raising a Series C and staying independent, scored on strategic fundamentals, not gut feel alone.

Prepared July 15, 2026 · Confidential board material
1

The real decision

The choice is between a $40M acquisition offer today and staying independent to raise a Series C for a shot at a materially larger outcome.

The real trade-off is certainty versus ceiling: one path locks in a known number, the other keeps the upside open at the cost of the guarantee.

It is emotionally hard because co-founders want liquidity now, while walking away from a top-of-market offer is very difficult to reverse.

2

The case for each option

Accept the acquisition

Strongest case

Locks in $40M at the top of the current market and removes execution risk for the whole team.

Hidden upside

Frees the founding team's time and capital to build the next thing without existential financial pressure.

Biggest weakness

Caps the outcome permanently. If the company was headed toward a much larger exit, that value is gone.

Best if you value: certainty and control over outcome

Stay independent, raise Series C

Strongest case

Preserves the option for a 5-10x larger outcome and matches what the founder's gut is already signaling.

Hidden upside

A fresh raise brings new investor scrutiny and discipline that often sharpens strategy faster than staying private ever would.

Biggest weakness

Raise risk is real. If the market turns, the company may end up worth far less than the offer on the table today.

Best if you value: long-term upside and momentum
3

Assumption buster

Assumptions likely in play
  • That a rejected offer of this size will come back later on similar terms. It usually does not.
  • That staying independent guarantees a bigger outcome. It only guarantees more time in the risk.
  • That co-founder pressure for liquidity reflects the company's actual trajectory, not just individual risk tolerance.
Biases at play
  • Loss aversion: the fear of regretting a rejected $40M offer is being weighted more heavily than the equally real cost of capping the upside.
  • Status quo bias: staying independent feels like "no decision," when in fact it is an active bet to keep building under raise risk.
What is being ignored: the cost of a slow, indecisive board process itself. Every week spent deliberating narrows the acquirer's patience and the fundraising window alike.

5

Premortem

Accept acquisition — fails by month 12

  • The team disperses post-acquisition and the product loses its original edge.
  • Founders regret capping an outcome that would have been far larger.
  • Integration friction erodes the deal's promised terms.

Early warning: key team members start leaving within the first two quarters.

Prevention: negotiate retention terms and product autonomy into the deal before signing.

Stay independent — fails by month 12

  • The Series C raise stalls or closes at a down round.
  • Market conditions shift, and the $40M offer is no longer on the table.
  • Cash runway gets dangerously short before the round closes.

Early warning: lead investor interest goes quiet for more than three weeks.

Prevention: set a hard runway floor and a go/no-go date before starting the raise.

6

7-day test plan

Day 1-2
Get real numbers: independent valuation range and current fundraising market conditions.
Day 3-4
Run one small experiment: soft-circle interest from one lead investor without a formal raise.
Day 5-6
Have one direct conversation with the acquirer to test if any deal flexibility exists.
Day 7
Decide: if investor interest is credible and the acquirer will not move, proceed toward the raise.
7

The verdict

Recommended path
Stay independent, raise Series C

It wins 45-39 on the matrix and matches the gut call already in play. The upside case is stronger than the safety case here.

What could flip it: if the 7-day investor test comes back cold, the acquisition becomes the safer, correct call.

Hard truth: the fear here is not the wrong decision, it is losing a guarantee. That is a different risk than losing the company.
