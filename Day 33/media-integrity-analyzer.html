Media Integrity Analyzer

An interactive media literacy learning tool built to teach headline analysis and emotional-manipulation detection through guided discovery.

A guided lesson

Learn to read media the way an analyst does.

This is not a quiz. Each section shows a piece of simulated content, invites your honest reaction, and then reveals what a careful reader would notice. Two short challenges. One dashboard at the end that reflects your session.

What you will practice

Spotting exaggerated headlines, and recognizing when language is designed to make you feel something before you have had a chance to think. Both skills apply directly to the news feed, the group chat forward, and the video you almost shared without reading.

Learning objectives

  • Distinguish a headline's claim from the evidence provided in the article body.
  • Identify specific words and phrases used to produce urgency, fear, or insecurity.
  • Interpret a composite media integrity score across four measurable dimensions.
  • Apply three transferable habits to real news and social content after the session.

Methodology

This tool follows a discover-then-reveal instructional design: the learner first forms an unguided reaction to simulated content, then compares that reaction against an expert-style analysis. This sequencing is intended to build durable judgment rather than memorized rules, consistent with inquiry-based approaches used in media literacy education. All headlines, articles, and posts are fictional and constructed for instructional purposes only.

Concept 1 of 2

Headlines are written to be clicked, not to be accurate.

01

Why this matters

A headline's job is to earn a click in under two seconds. That incentive rewards exaggeration, vague sourcing, and emotionally loaded words, sometimes at the direct expense of what the article actually reports. Reading the headline as a claim, and the article as the evidence for that claim, is the single habit that catches the most misleading content.

Concept 2 of 2

Language can trigger emotion before you engage your judgment.

02

Why this matters

Fear, outrage, and moral disgust make content spread faster because they demand an immediate reaction. Recognizing which specific words are doing that work, separate from the underlying facts, lets you respond to the substance instead of the emotional trigger. This is the same skill used to evaluate political ads, health scares, and viral posts.

Three habits to carry forward

  • 1Read the headline as a claim. Find the sentence in the article that proves or disproves it before you form an opinion.
  • 2Notice the first emotion a piece of content produces. Name it, then ask what specific word or image caused it.
  • 3Check who benefits from you feeling that emotion right now, and who the content seems built to reach.
